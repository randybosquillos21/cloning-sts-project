What is Cloning? (STS Lens)

Scientific Definition

Cloning is the process of generating genetically identical copies of a biological entity — from a single gene, a cell, or an entire organism. In molecular biology, it often refers to recombinant DNA technology, while reproductive cloning produces whole multicellular organisms.

Societal & Technological View

In STS (Science, Technology and Society), cloning is analyzed not just as a lab technique but as a site of ethical controversy, media representation, regulatory battles, and cultural anxiety about “playing God,” identity, and commodification of life.

Historical Context

From 1950s frog cloning to Dolly the Sheep (1996) — each milestone triggered public debate, reshaping bioethics and law. STS scholars study how scientific progress and social values co-evolve around cloning technology.

How Cloning Works : Core Techniques

1

Nuclear Transfer (SCNT)

Somatic Cell Nuclear Transfer: Remove nucleus from an egg cell, insert nucleus from a donor somatic cell. Electric/chemical fusion initiates embryonic development.

2

Artificial Embryo Activation

The reconstructed egg is stimulated to divide like a normal embryo, forming a blastocyst. Implanted into a surrogate mother for reproductive cloning.

3

Molecular Cloning (Gene Cloning)

DNA fragment inserted into a plasmid vector → transformed into bacteria → copied via cell division. Used in labs for protein production & gene therapy.

Detailed Insight: Reproductive vs. Therapeutic

Reproductive cloning aims to create a whole organism (like Dolly). Therapeutic cloning produces embryonic stem cells for regenerative medicine — without implantation. Both share SCNT but diverge in purpose and ethical weight. Modern advances include induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs) as an alternative.

"Cloning efficiency remains low (~1-5% success in mammals), and cloned animals often exhibit epigenetic abnormalities." – Wilmut et al., Nature 1997.

Different Kinds of Cloning

TypeDescriptionApplications & Examples
Gene Cloning (Molecular)Amplifying specific DNA sequences using vectors & host cells (E. coli).Protein production, gene therapy, DNA sequencing. Insulin production via recombinant DNA.
Reproductive CloningProduction of a whole organism genetically identical to donor.Dolly the sheep (1996), cloned cows, pigs for agriculture, endangered species (like Przewalski's horse).
Therapeutic CloningEmbryo creation via SCNT to harvest stem cells (up to blastocyst stage).Regenerative medicine, research on Parkinson's, spinal cord injury. Not for live birth.
Natural Cloning (Twins)Monozygotic twins share identical DNA, considered natural clones.Human identical twins, asexual reproduction in some plants/animals (e.g., budding in hydra).

Emerging & Special Cases

Pet cloning: Commercial services (e.g., ViaGen) clone cats, dogs for grieving owners — cost $35k–$50k. De-extinction: Projects aiming to resurrect mammoth or thylacine using ancient DNA + cloning techniques. Strong STS debates on ecological & moral risks.

STS: Cloning as a Socio-Scientific Issue

Ethical & Legal Dimensions

Human dignity: Is a clone a full person? Many countries ban human reproductive cloning (UN declaration 2005). Therapeutic cloning allowed in UK, South Korea, but restricted elsewhere. Religious views: Catholic Church opposes all human cloning because it separates procreation from marital union.

Media & Public Perception

After Dolly, headlines triggered "cloning panic". Films like 'The Island' and 'Never Let Me Go' reinforce fears of organ farming. STS scholars examine how sci-fi influences policy and funding allocations.

Social Justice & Access

Who benefits from cloning? High-tech agriculture, pharmaceutical industries, wealthy pet owners — potential widening inequality. Low success rates and animal suffering also raise animal welfare concerns.

STS Frameworks applied: Actor-Network Theory views cloning as a network of scientists, regulators, ethical committees, and non-humans (DNA, enzymes). Technological determinism vs. Social construction — cloning's meaning shaped by cultural context rather than pure science.
